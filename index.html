About

Water is one of the most essential things to life. We need water to live, however unfortunately, billions of people around the world do not have access to clean water to drink and for sanitization. This is of course a horrible problem which we have decided to investigate. Water insecurity has many negative impacts on the communities it affects. Lack of water or clean water can deteriorate health (both physical and mental), in some instances resulting in death, makes food prices higher, disproportionately affects women leading to women being uneducated, etc. All of these factors combined constrain economic growth, leaving large communities to be unable to develop in further areas, as the focus has to be on survival.

Women in Malawi

The settlement of Ntopwa in Malawi, Africa, is one of the many communities in the world that faces water insecurity. For the people of Ntopwa, there are many risks that arise due to the lack of access to clean water. Women in this community are often the ones tasked with traveling long distances in order to bring back water for their families. They’re are many dangerous risks extended to environmental, physical, and social factors. Some environmental factors include the risk of slipping on terrain, falling, drowning, injury on sharp objects, mosquitos, and road-crossing. Beyond that, water can be contaminated, leading to disease and an increased mortality rate. One of the biggest social downfalls aspects for women in the Ntopwa community is the missed education due to having to fetch water for their families. In turn, women missing out on education also miss out on working outside the home, ending up with them not being able to pursue a career and grow in socioeconomic status by themselves.

In the Yucatan Peninsula the correlation of lack of accessibility to clean water affects the happiness and wellbeing of the people in this region, especially women. The lack of clean water leads to diseases such as diarrhea and cholera, these two diseases correlate to 5% of the total deaths in the world. The direct impact that the water insecurity has on women is the infant death rate in Yucatan is at 12.6 deaths per 1000 infants, this is more than double the average infant death rate in America which is 5.54. The lack of clean water leads to mothers not being able to properly feed and nurture their children which results in undernutrition, dehydration, as well as hunger. Which ultimately contributes to the infant mortality rate. In regards to the well being of all people the lack of sufficient water results in non sanitary drinking water which leads to many diseases and other health risks. As well as unsanitary water for other uses such as bathing and hygiene also contribute to many diseases and health risks.

The Water Crisis in Brazil

This image shows us how the water crisis is affecting different areas in Brazil. In Brazil they have been having trouble with a uniform water resource distribution. Because of these problems this is impacting the economy. Without freshwater the production of goods is slowed and the power generation is lacking. The emerging technology that may be an alternative to reduce problems of availability to get clean drinking water would be desolation. Desolation has been used in different parts of the world and has been a success but it is expensive and requires much energy. Brazil has started the process of desolation though and has 590 systems already implemented throughout the country covering about 230 thousand people. Women are going to benefit from this due to the fact that it's reducing the strain of household tasks. Before they had pipes that ran to their homes, they would go to get it on foot and carry old breakable buckets over their heads. It wouldn’t just be the women, it would be the children too. Thanks to the piped water women have more time to rest and they are in less pain and agony. The water desolation is helping so many families in Brazil.

How The Lack of Freshwater Affects Women in Vietnam

Lack of freshwater has proved to be a major problem for people living in marginalized communities in Vietnam, including women. The shocking thing I discovered is the articles I read had very little mention on malnourishment and dehydration, but a heavy emphasis on the mental health effects regarding the lack of freshwater. The article stated that the amount of water at disposal is associated with mental health levels. It also concluded the amount of freshwater readily available is determined by socioeconomic status and gave the following as evidence; 94% of urban and 98% of wealthy communities have access to freshwater whereas 78% of rural communities have access, 61% of ethnic minority enhabited areas have freshwater, and 41% of less fortunate areas didn't have access. Those numbers may not seem like a lot, but in comparison 97.33% of Americans have access to readily available freshwater. These people are put at a staggering disadvantage that not only causes malnourishment and dehydration, but causes severe anxiety and depression.
